Ex.1 Parsing Protein Structures

Published

April 3, 2026

1 Introduction

Proteins are macromolecules whose function is highly related to their three-dimensional structure. Their organization can be described in four levels, which depend on folding. Protein folding is driven by non-covalent intramolecular interactions between residues of the same polypeptide chain, whereas intermolecular interactions contribute to quaternary structure formation. These interactions include hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions and electrostatic forces. Structural bioinformatics aims to characterize these interactions through computational analysis of experimentally determined or predicted structures.

The main repository of experimentally determined macromolecuar structures is the Protein Data Bank (PDB), which stores three-dimensional structures obtained by X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy and cryo-electron microscopy. PDB files contain detailed metadata, experimental information, protein sequences, and three-dimensional atomic coordinates. Through computational analysis, this information can be extracted from these files.

To analyze protein architecture contact maps are commonly used as a simplified representation of the three-dimensional structure. These maps use pairwise distance matrices to provide a two-dimensional visualization of the interactions between the Cα atoms of residues within a protein or between different protein chains. In addition, specific patterns are associated with specific secondary structure elements, so it is possible to infer the spatial organization of proteins.

Nowadays, although modelling and structure prediction methods have achieved high levels of accuracy, the analysis of atomic coordinates is still essential to understand the spatial organization of proteins and macromolecular complexes.

1.1 Objective

The main objective of this project is to implement computational functions to:

  1. Parse PDB files to extract structural metadata and atomic coordinates.
  2. Compute pairwise distance matrices between Cα atoms of protein residues.
  3. Plot contact maps to characterize the structural organization and oligomeric state of the analyzed proteins.

2 Methods

2.1 Data Collection

RCSB-PDB (Research Collaboratory for Structural Bioinformatics Protein Data Bank, https://www.rcsb.org/) is a repository of experimentally determined, and also computationally predicted, structures of proteins, nucleic acids and macromolecular complexes.

Each protein in this database is identified with a unique four-character alphanumeric identifier. This identifier allows access to detailed information about the protein, such as atomic coordinates, sequence, experimental methodology and associated metadata.

The structures analyzed in this project correspond to the following proteins:

  • Plasma Retinol-Binding Protein Precursor (Homo sapiens, PDB ID: 1RBP), which is involved in retinol transport in humans.
  • Carbonic Anhydrase (Methanosarcina thermophila, PDB ID: 1QRE), which regulates pH by catalyzing the reaction between \(CO_2\) and \(H_2O\) to produce bicarbonate and protons.
  • Catalase (Homo sapiens, PDB ID: 1DGF), which protect cells from oxidative damage by decomposing \(H_2O_2\) into water and oxygen.

2.3 Structural Infrormation Extraction and Distance Matrix Calculation

To understand how a protein folds and how its redidues interact with each other, we first need to extract the spatial coordinates of the amino acids. This allows us to calculate the pairwise distances between them.

PDB stablished a standardized, human-readable text format to store the 3D structures of biomolecules. Each line in a PDB file corresponds to a specific type of record, utilizing fixed-width columns. The file begins with a header section, which contains general information about the structure, such as the PDB ID, protein name, realesed date, etc. This is followed by the atomic coordinates section, where each line corresponds to an atom in the structure and contains information about its type, residue, chain, and 3D coordinates (x, y, z). Those lines start with the keyword “ATOM”. Finally, there is a connectivity section that describes how these atoms are bonded to form the overall structure of the molecule.

To extract the mentioned data, we have implemented the function Function 5, which make a request to the RCSB PDB API to obtain raw textual data of the PDB file. It then parses the response to extract the atomic coordinates of the C\(\alpha\) atoms, which are commonly used to represent the backbone of the protein structure.

The function also, translates the three-letter amino acid codes into one-letter codes using a mapping dictionary. Finally, it extracts the chain identifier, residue number and the cartesian coordinates (x, y, z), returning a structured dataframe that will be used for subsequent distance and contact map calculations.

To understand a protein’s three-dimensional structure, we must measure the distances between its different parts. A Distance Matrix is a useful tool for this, providing a complete view of how every pair of residues in the structure is positioned. By calculating these distances, we can identify secondary structural elements and determine the tertiary or quaternary folding patterns that are crucial for the protein’s biological function.

Mathematically, since each amino acid is represented by the Cartesian coordinates \((x, y, z)\) of its alpha carbon (\(C\alpha\)), the distance \(d\) between two residues \(i\) and \(j\) is calculated using the three-dimensional Euclidean distance formula: \[d_{ij} = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j)^2 + (y_i - y_j)^2 + (z_i - z_j)^2}\]

To compute this, we implemented the Function 6, which extracts the \((x, y, z)\) coordinates as a NumPy array for optimized access. Using a nested loop structure, it iterates through every possible pair of atoms, calculating the differences in each spatial dimension, with the result in Angstroms (Å).

2.4 Contact Map Calculation

In proteins, there can be both intrachain and interchain contacts, taking that into account, Function 7 function was implemented to generate the corresponding matrices from the pairwise Cα distance matrix and residue chain information.

Contacts were defined using a specific distance threshold, set by default between 6 Å and 12 Å. Distances below 6 Å are excluded to avoid trivial sequential contacts (immediate neighbors along the backbone), while distance above 12 Å are generally too far for meaningful non-covalent interactions. This range captures the most relevant spatial interactions associated with protein folding and function.

To visualize the computed contact matrices of each protein Function 8 function was developed to generate contact maps differentiating between intrachain and interchain interactions, represented in green and red, respectively, which makes it easier the identification of oligomers.

A key feature of this function is its ability to isolate and analyze contact maps for specific protein chains using the ‘chains’ argument, which significantly streamlines the visualization of complex structures.

3.2 Distance Matrix

Structural information allows to calculate the distance matrix. As mentioned before, the distance matrix contains the distance between carbon-alpha (\(C\alpha\)) atoms of each amino acid one-to-one. This is calculated as the 3D Euclidean distance between the coordinates \((x, y, z)\) of the atoms.

On the diagonal, the values are equal to 0 because the amino acids being compared are the same. The matrix provides a quantitative map of the protein’s spatial organization. Values near the diagonal represent local interactions, reflecting the proximity of residues in the primary sequence. Conversely, off-diagonal values represent long-range interactions, identifying residues that are distant in the sequence but spatially close due to the three-dimensional folding of the polypeptide chain.

In multimeric proteins, this matrix also reveals the quaternary interface. By observing the distances between residues belonging to different chain identifiers, we can pinpoint the specific regions where subunits pack together. This matrix serves as the mathematical foundation for the binary contact maps, as it allows us to apply specific distance thresholds to filter out significant biological interactions.

To focus on biologically relevant interactions, the distance matrices were filtered using a threshold of 6–12 Å. This range ensures that only significant spatial contacts—those contributing to protein folding and stability—are retained. The values out of range are represented as \(NaN\).

Through the plot_contact_map function, the data was partitioned into two distinct matrices: one containing the intra-chain contact distances and the other the inter-chain contact distances. The first one, the intra_contact_matrix, captures the internal distances between residues belonging to the same polypeptide chain, representing the protein’s tertiary fold. The second one, the inter_contact_matrix, allows for the identification of spatial relationships between residues from different chains. It is essential for characterizing the quaternary structure and pinpointing the interfaces where separate subunits interact to form multimeric assemblies.

This dual-matrix approach allows for a clear visual and numerical distinction between the folding of individual subunits and the stabilization of the overall protein complex.

3.3 Contact Map Analysis

The generated contact maps provide a spatial representation of the protein’s fold and quaternary structure using a distance threshold of 6–12 Å. The visualization distinguishes between two fundamental types of interactions:

  • Intra-chain contacts: These represent the internal folding of individual polypeptide chains in green. The clusters observed along the diagonal correspond to secondary structure elements (such as \(\alpha\)-helices) and tertiary packing within the subunit.

  • Inter-chain contacts: These highlight the interfaces between different subunits. The presence of red clusters indicates the quaternary organization of the protein, marking specific interaction surfaces that stabilize the complex.

3.3.1 Quaternary Structure Interpretation

Based on the distribution of contacts and the chain boundary indicators (dashed lines), different structural features were identified. On the one hand, monomeric structures which are characterized by the exclusive presence of green contacts within a single quadrant. On the other hand, multimeric assemblies (homo- or hetero-dimers, tetramers, etc.) which are identified by the appearance of red contact patterns between distinct chain segments.

The implementation of np.equal.outer and plt.imshow with \(NaN\) masking allowed for the clear overlay of these two interaction types, facilitating the identification of protein interfaces and overall topology.

The 1RBP protein contact map (Figure 1 (a)) shows discrete segments oriented perpendicularly to the main diagonal. These patterns are the classic “signature” of antiparallel \(\beta\)-sheets, which in this case constitute the eight-stranded \(\beta\)-barrel that forms the protein’s central cavity. The regular spacing between the off-diagonal clusters indicates the highly organized hydrogen-bonding network between adjacent \(\beta\)-strands. The contacts between the first and the last strands are also visible as distal signals. This represents the closed barrel structure. Furthermore, in the C-terminal region, the main diagonal exhibits a distinct thickening, which represents the presence of an \(\alpha\)-helical segment in this specific area.

The 1QRE contact map (Figure 1 (b)) exhibits prominent clusters of contacts running parallel to the main diagonal. These patterns represent parallel \(\beta\)-sheets, which characterize the central core of the protein’s fold. A significant thickening is observed along the entire main diagonal. This indicates a high density of \((i, i+4)\) local interactions, representing the presence of multiple \(\alpha\)-helices packed against the central sheets. The plot displays three clearly defined blocks of contacts, within which several segments run parallel to the main diagonal; these patterns indicate the structural packing and close-range interactions between the \(\alpha\)-helices and \(\beta\)-sheets

Finally, if inter-chain distances are included, discrete contact zones appear between the three different chain identifiers. This represents the quaternary structure, specifically the trimeric interfaces where the three subunits interact and assemble to form the functional protein complex.

The 1DGF contact map (Figure 1 (c)) reveals prominent segments oriented both parallel and perpendicular to the main diagonal. This dual pattern represents a complex \(\alpha/\beta\) architecture, where the parallel lines indicate parallel \(\beta\)-sheets and the perpendicular segments confirm the presence of antiparallel \(\beta\)-strands within the same fold. A dense thickening is observed along the entire main diagonal, indicating the presence of multiple \(\alpha\)-helices, which are characterized by a high frequency of \((i, i+4)\) local interactions.

This pattern is consistently manifested across the diagonal quadrants of the contact matrix; since this pattern repeats four times with high regularity, it suggests that the protein is composed of four identical subunits with the same structural composition. The contacts displayed in the off-diagonal quadrants (indicated in red) represent the interactions between the different polypeptide chains—consistently with the metadata identifying a tetrameric structure—exhibiting a symmetric distribution along the main diagonal. Mapping the chains from bottom-to-top and left-to-right as A, B, C, and D allows for a direct comparison of inter-chain interactions. The analysis reveals that the highest density of contacts occurs between pairs A-B, A-C, B-D, and D-C, whereas a significantly lower number of contacts is observed in the quadrants corresponding to the A-D and B-C interfaces.

4 Discussion

This project has successfully implemented a computational approach to analyze protein architecture through the generation and interpretation of distance and contact matrices. By processing structural data from the PDB, we have achieved an efficient generation of contact maps, providing a clear visual representation of the complex three-dimensional folding of the studied proteins.

In particular, the developed pipeline was applied to characterize the intramolecular and intermolecular interaction patterns of proteins 1RBP, 1QRE, and 1DGF, effectively mapping their tertiary and quaternary organizations.

4.1 1RBP

The analysis of 1RBP (Retinol-Binding Protein) confirms its characteristic \(\beta\)-barrel tertiary structure. When compared to the reference data in the Protein Data Bank (PDB), where the structure was determined via X-ray diffraction at a high resolution of 2.0 Å, the generated contact map (Figure 1 (a)) precisely aligns with the experimentally observed conformation. The contact matrix highlights the \(\beta\)-barrel core where predominant anti-diagonal signals correspond to the eight antiparallel \(\beta\)-strands that wrap into a closed barrel, a hallmark of the lipocalin family. In addition to the barrel, the map identifies a specific \(\alpha\)-helical zone located near the C-terminus. This is visible in the contact map as a localized area of increased density along the main diagonal, representing the short-range \((i, i+4)\) interactions characteristic of helical turns.

4.2 1QRE

The second one, the 1QRE contact map (Figure 1 (b)) displays three clearly defined blocks of contacts which, in comparison with the protein structure resolved via X-ray diffraction at a high resolution of 1.46 Å, correspond to the three subunits observed in the PDB tertiary structure. Within these blocks, several segments run parallel to the main diagonal, indicating the structural packing between \(\alpha\)-helices and \(\beta\)-sheets. Finally, the discrete contact zones appearing between different chain identifiers represent the quaternary structure, specifically the trimeric interfaces where the three subunits interact to form the functional protein complex

Despite the metadata retrieved from the PDB indicating only a single chain, a comparison between the contact map and the PDB 3D structure view suggests the presence of three subunits with identical folds. This observation implies that the functional protein is likely a homotrimer, where the quaternary structure is composed of three identical subunits that were not explicitly detailed as such in the initial metadata.

4.3 1DGF

The last one, the 1DGE contact map (Figure 1 (c)) reveals a quaternary structure composed of four identical subunits, identifying it as a homotetramer. This observation is highly consistent with the experimentally resolved structure obtained via X-ray diffraction at a high resolution of 1.5 Å. Both the 3D visualization in the PDB and the contact map exhibit a fold characterized by antiparallel \(\beta\)-sheets organized into a barrel structure. These structural elements are packed against \(\alpha\)-helices, which further facilitate inter-helical interactions. Furthermore, the PDB data illustrates how the monomers interact; they show a higher density of contacts with adjacent subunits and a significantly lower degree of contact with the monomer located diagonally across the assembly. Consequently, the patterns depicted in the contact map align perfectly with the experimental structure archived in the PDB.

Consequently, contact maps (Figure 1) provide a comprehensive visualization of how distinct protein regions interact and reveal the specific secondary and tertiary structural motifs that compose them. As demonstrated throughout this project, these maps effectively reflect the organizational complexity of monomeric, trimeric, and tetrameric assemblies.

Furthermore, contact maps facilitate the identification of recurring structural patterns and the characterization of inter-chain contacts within the quaternary structure. The close agreement between these contact maps and the high-resolution, experimentally resolved structures archived in the PDB underscores the utility of distance-based matrices for elucidating the intricate interactions and architectures of proteins.

5 Conclusion

In this project, we have successfully developed a robust computational pipeline to analyze protein structures through distance and contact matrices. By leveraging Python and structural data from the PDB, we achieved the efficient generation of contact maps, providing a clear visual representation of complex three-dimensional folds. A critical aspect of this work was the implementation of a coherent distance filtering strategy. By applying thresholds between 6 and 12 Å, we effectively eliminated structural noise, retaining only the biologically significant interactions. This allowed for a precise distinction between intra-chain (tertiary) and inter-chain (quaternary) contacts, pinpointing the exact regions responsible for subunit packing and overall complex stabilization.The results demonstrate that contact maps are powerful tools for structural biology, as they condense thousands of atomic coordinates into identifiable geometric patterns—such as the characteristic signatures of \(\alpha\)-helices and \(\beta\)-sheets. In conclusion, the methodologies applied in this study provide a reliable and scalable framework for the comparative analysis of protein architectures and their functional interfaces.
